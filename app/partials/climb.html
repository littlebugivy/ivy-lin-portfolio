*Note: this work is part of the research project “Climb!” carried out by composer and pianist Maria Kallionpää in collaboration with Mixed Reality Laboratory of Nottingham University and the Sound and Music Knowledge Group of Aalborg University. Complete design and development processes are illustrated in a publication which I co-authored and is accepted by CHI conference in 2018.

Background Introduction

Musicians have continuously been exploring the possibilities of presenting innovative musical experiences to their audiences. One of the pathways that they have gone done is the possibility of integration modern technologies, especially computational technologies, into music performance and production. In this project, Dr. Kallionpää introduced a game-like musical composition, Climb!, with the intention to explore and understand the interaction between computer generated and human-performed piano pieces, in which the interaction may bring new experiences and interpretation of music to the audience.

As the name of the composition, Climb!, indicates, the musician goes from basecamp to summit (Fig1). There are three paths with mulitple events and paths on the way, each of which represents one micro piano composition. The arrowed lines connecting stages are possible destinations of that stage. By performing differently, the musician determines the path, or say, experience that audiences go through, which leads to various versions of performances as a whole. The composition also involves a Disklavier piano, which performs automatically accompanying the musician as pre-defined.

Needs Identification

Through contexual inquiry with comprehensive field studies (attending three rehearsals), it is reliased that the performance is difficult to be interpreted. What's more, it causes confusion to audiences due to its complex ‘magical’ interactions. On the other hand, the innovative stucture of the composition is unpercivable to audiences. For example, different stages are undistinguishable from each other and their stories are not presented, which, if being revealed, could very likely raise more interests.

Therefore, at this stage the user needs are identified as:
        1. Need a better understanding of the entire performance setting.
        2. Need a better understanding of the hidden structure of the composition.

Design Process

1. First Phase of Design

The assisted tool is developed to be a web-based mobile application. Different from building native mobile applications, a web application allows rapid and flexible iterations, meanwhile, prevent users from downloading. Insipred by the paper programme notes used in traditional music concerts, the first phase of design incorporated a dynamic map. (See video below, in 3x speed) The appreance of the system adopts a mountain look to meet the leitmotiv of the performacne. Each colored dot represent one micro-composition and they are revealed one by one as the performance progress up the mountain. In each stage, a short narritive will be revealed to further introduce that context.

One thing worths noting is that the design aims at scaffold understanding and interpretation of the work rather than to unpick audiences' specific interpretations.

2. User Testing - Premiere

The premiere of Climb! took place in a classical recital hall as part of a regular series of contemporary music recitals at the University of Nottingham. The composer performed the work herself and around 50 audiences attended. The audience was a mixture of regular attendees for the event and those who had been invited by the research team. The regular attendees had a specific interest in contemporary music. Other attendees had much more diverse (mainstream) musical preferences, but most attendees reported at least “good” expertise in music. Video



The experiment was designed as two run in the premiere. In the first round, audiences were given minimized information about the performance with the mobile application, while in the second round we offered a vocal introduction about the performance including its unique structure and special settings, and introduce how the mobile application connected to the performance. Paper questionnaires were given after each round of performance and interviews were conducted on five selected audiences. In two performances the musician chose different paths.

3. Second Phase of Design

We analyzed data collected from questionnaires and interviews. [quotes from users] After reflecting on analysis results and further discussions with Dr. Kallionpää, more needs are revealed regarding to the mobile application:
               1. Incorporate introduction about the performance and allows customization
               2. Allow to compare and contrast performances to suit the repetitive nature of the composition
To meet these needs, the second phase of design was designed and developed.

The second phase of design was incorporated in the second performance in London in Augest after I leaving the lab. :(
